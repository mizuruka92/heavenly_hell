It was a bright day in Asgard — birds chirping, flowers rising, the sun still far away yet greeting us softly. The sun looked small and dim, but the magical powers and mana streams cast their light upon us in this hidden land no common mortal could see.

I could roughly see about twenty moons out of the hundreds that circled Asgard, and the faint tint of the icy bridge where the Bifrost leaked out of the realm. Asgard, by default, was a bit chilly — not that it affected my rotten bones — but I, the introverted princess, would rather stay under my blanket in one of the rooms in Valaskjalf assigned to me.

Unlike my two brothers who were very resistant to cold, my flesh was a little more sensitive. Nevertheless, I was strong and would always be strong. I believed I was even stronger than the so-called strongest maiden in all of Asgard — though the word maiden was questionable, considering she was the Queen of Asgard.

I heard footsteps, ending in a knock at my door.
“Pardon me, Lady Anabel, your breakfast is ready,” said one of the castle maids.

I did not answer. She left the tray on the table in front of my door, and I listened to her steps fade before I opened it.

As expected — the usual Sæhrímnir, said to be the best pork in the nine realms. Today it was rolled and grilled, the skin crispy and a little burnt. I brought it into my room and ate in silence. Then I gulped my Heiðrún milk, placed the tray back outside (after ensuring no one was wandering around), and returned to my bed with my books.

My father frequently visited Midgard and often brought back books from there for me. It should have been forbidden, but he somehow had his ways. He was a respected god after all — though perhaps respected in a more negative way. My father certainly loved me the most, for I was his youngest child and only daughter. He had other children with other women besides my mother, but I was his only girl. So, naturally, he delighted in bringing me gifts to accompany my self-granted isolation.

This time, it was a story about a girl granted magic by a pair of shoes that sparkled like mirrors. I peeked under my blanket, looking at my own feet — rotten like a decaying corpse, horrendous at best, gore at worst — but that was what defined me.

Yet I was quite proud of my face: flesh on skin intact, and some even said it was fair, with bosoms to boast.

I was the living and the dead at the same time.
I am Anabel.
