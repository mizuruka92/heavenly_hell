It was long past bedtime for the Asgardians. The night was cold; its chill could pierce any bones — but mine came from something far worse. Uneasiness filled me. I stood and listened, far from where the Halls of Valhalla should stand.

I rarely went out unless summoned, or when something truly important arose — though I always dismissed such things as never important.
I heard weeping. I heard whispers. I heard people badmouthing me. I heard my brother growling, longing to be freed. Yet again, I did not step outside.

I knew it was none of my business. But if curiosity kills the cat, then I was its killer — for curious I am, and my heart could not rest. A cry. A girl. A man’s laughter. Only a liar would dismiss the thought that something wicked had happened; only a fool would say they could not connect the dots. These things were plain to anyone.

Youth I may still have, but in human years, thousands had passed beneath my decaying legs — an age still young for a goddess, yet not too young for me to act.

I stopped, hand on the doorknob. I gathered my courage — and failed — until I realized that if I hesitated any longer, I would move beyond my regrets. So I went out, silent but swift, toward the Halls of Valhalla.

The hall was dead silent. The feast had ended; the guards were lost in their drunken joy. My ghostly presence went unnoticed — but one weeping sound still echoed in my ears. It rang loud, as if it had never known the word “help,” yet pleaded for one.
And that was the sound my instinct chose to follow.
