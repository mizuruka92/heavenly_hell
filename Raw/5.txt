The hall was dead silent. The feast had ended; the guards were lost in their drunken joy. My ghostly presence went unnoticed — but one weeping sound still echoed in my ears. It rang loud, as if it had never known the word “help,” yet pleaded for one. And that was the sound my instinct chose to follow.

Then I saw her. A girl with long dark hair curled up in the darkest corner of Valhalla. Blank. Her clothes were torn. Blood between her legs — the deed was done. It took me a while to realize that her sobbing had ended, though her tears had not and kept flowing through her bruised face. Her cries had dried by the time I reached her. She held a knife, and I knew what she had tried to do — but she hadn’t found the courage.

I gathered my own courage to walk toward her. I was afraid of how she might react, but standing still would have been foolish.
Her eyes followed my presence, yet not a word escaped her lips. She knew me. Everyone knew me — Anabel the Decayed. Someone to be feared. Someone to be loathed. Someone to be despised.


I kneeled before her. She didn’t even blink; her gaze was empty. I didn’t know what to ask. Even “what happened” would have been foolish.

“What do you wish?” I asked. I could hear my voice trembling as I never spoke to anyone for a very long time. But the fact that the girl was broken added more layers to my fear of saying the wrong things.
She gave me a hollow stare.

I took a deep breath. “Johann, wasn’t it?” I continued, recognizing the laughter that had woken me earlier.

She didn’t answer, but her tears flowed again — more fiercely this time. I cursed myself. If I hadn’t been asleep, I might have heard the assault sooner. Perhaps I could have stopped it. Or perhaps not, as I rarely ever left my room. I was no hero — and I would never be.

I knew the girl, or rather I knew every single person in Asgard, through their voices, their breath, their presence. I heard every person's voice everyday including hers, though she did not say anything that time. However, even though I never saw her face, I knew her presence and I could tell from my memories which voice belonged to her. She was one of the many maids employed by Freya for the nightly feasts

“Garaam, wasn’t it?” I asked. She did not reply.
“I know where you live,” I said.

Her clothes were in tatters — she was too exposed to walk outside. I wrapped my shawl around her.

I tried to remove the knife from her hand, however she held it so tight in contrast to her hollow will. I gave up.
“Keep the knife,” I told her. “But mind you, you will not be needing this.”

She said nothing, but her glance was set towards the knife on her hand.

“I will take you back to where you belong. For now just sleep,” I told her softly as I cast a spell on her.
“When you wake up, I will bring good news.”.

I was not someone who cared about others, or maybe I cared but never acted.
