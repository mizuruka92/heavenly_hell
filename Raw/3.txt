Midgard had twenty-four hours in a day, which made the nights long.
The sphere where Asgard stood, on the other hand, had by default only ten hours of combined day and night. Yet, as we lived through mana, the sun never truly shone upon our days.
Instead, the mana created light that lasted roughly twelve hours, and a warm darkness that lingered for another twelve.
Thus, our days and nights never determined the cycle of our calendar, for there were mana nights when the dim sun could still be seen far away.


No one had ever met the Creator, yet His ancient magic shaped the world — the universe itself.
Even we could see the larger cosmos far across the sky, like a great pinwheel turning in the void.
Even a goddess like me wondered if there was life beyond it.

I enjoyed my nights reading the books my father brought me before sleep.
Valhalla was full of cheer as the warriors reveled in their feast.
I could hear every whisper, every laughter, every word — my hearing was astronomical, though my form was not as grand as my two brothers’.
But my most precious gift was my memory.
I could recall every sight and every sound for longer than eternity — the tones, the voices, even the faintest pressure of sound.
I remembered faces and their voices, and even from afar, in the hall of Valhalla, I could distinguish who was who, though I did not know their names.

The book I read that night was quite boring, yet once I began, I had to finish.
Still, it became its own challenge as my eyes grew heavy and my will faltered.
Soon, I fell asleep — though, like all others, one only knows they have slept once they awaken.
And when I woke, it was to the sound of weeping — a deep wound made audible — and I learned that misery was the true name of tears.
